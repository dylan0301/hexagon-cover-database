\section{Overview, notation, and main result}
\label{sec:introduction}

This chapter gives the reader the entire proof architecture before any long
calculation.  It introduces the geometric objects and case labels, states the
exhaustive routing table, and explains the four mechanisms that close its
rows.  Section~\ref{sec:structural-reductions} then proves that these labels
and reductions are exhaustive.  The complete algebra supporting the four
mechanisms is collected after the final assembly in
Appendices~\ref{app:strategy1-details}--\ref{app:strategy4-details}.

\subsection{The theorem}

Let $H$ be the regular hexagon of side length one.

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem]
\label{thm:main}
The hexagon $H$ cannot be covered by seven open equilateral triangles of
side length one.
\end{theorem}

The compactness and scaling argument in
Proposition~\ref{prop:open-closed-scaled} gives the equivalent closed form.

\begin{corollary}[Expanded closed formulation]
\label{cor:expanded-closed}
For every $L>1$, the regular hexagon $H_L$ of side length $L$ cannot be
covered by seven closed unit equilateral triangles.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{A geometric dictionary}
\label{subsec:reader-dictionary}

Indices are taken in $\mathbb Z/6\mathbb Z$.  Put
\[
 O=(0,0),\qquad
 V_i=\left(\cos\frac{i\pi}{3},\sin\frac{i\pi}{3}\right),\qquad
 H=\conv\{V_0,\ldots,V_5\}.
\]
Write $e_{i,i+1}=[V_i,V_{i+1}]$ for a boundary edge,
$r_i=[O,V_i]$ for a radial arm, and $M_i=V_i/2$.  The radial target and the
full skeleton are
\[
 D=\bigcup_{i=0}^5r_i,\qquad S=\partial H\cup D,
\]
with lengths $\mathcal H^1(\partial H)=\mathcal H^1(D)=6$ and
$\mathcal H^1(S)=12$.

Seven distinguished points force seven distinct roles: $U_C$ contains $O$
and $U_i$ contains $V_i$.  Their closures are denoted by $T_C$ and $T_i$.
The distinction is essential because closing an open trace may add an
endpoint or a tangency.

For a vertex role, let $o$ be the number of its triangle vertices outside
$H$ and $n$ the number of adjacent arms met in positive length.  The
exhaustive names are
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{type}&(o,n)\\ \hline
\Vdzero&(1,0),(2,0),(3,0)\\
\Vdone&(1,1)\\
\Vdtwo&(1,2)\\
\Tlike&(2,1).
\end{array}
\]
These classifications are mathematical rather than pictorial; the proofs of
exhaustiveness appear in
Propositions~\ref{prop:ce-classification} and
\ref{prop:vertex-classification}.

For an original open vertex role, let $A_i,B_i,C_i$ be its actual maximal
incoming boundary, outgoing boundary, and inward radial reaches.  Thus row
$i$ is \emph{supercritical} when $A_i+B_i>1$, and
\[
 N_+=\left\lvert\left\{i:A_i+B_i>1\right\}\right\rvert.
\]
Lowercase $(a_i,b_i,c_i)$ will always denote smaller selected demands, never
the actual maxima used to define $N_+$.

On $e_{i,i+1}$, parametrized from $V_i$ to $V_{i+1}$, the two incident open
traces are $[0,B_i)$ and $(1-A_{i+1},1]$.  Their nonempty complement
\[
 [B_i,1-A_{i+1}]
\]
is a boundary gap.  Equality gives a singleton gap, which remains uncovered
because both incident traces are open at that point.

\subsection{The four strategies}

\paragraph{Strategy 1: one-dimensional trace budgets.}
Choose one of $\partial H$, $D$, or the conditional skeleton $S$.  Bound the
trace of each role on that target according to its center class, vertex type,
and supercritical status.  In every routed branch the seven upper bounds add
to strictly less than the target length.  The target, rather than the case
label, is the organizing idea.

\paragraph{Strategy 2: exact demand propagation.}
A center trace supplies boundary inputs and complementary radial demands.
For a nonsupercritical vertex row, a monotone capped map converts an incoming
boundary demand into a lower bound on the next row.  Iterating around the
hexagon returns to row $0$ with a demand exceeding its exact remaining
capacity.  The body displays the actual-row propagation mechanism; the
individual five-link inequalities, piecewise map,
rational estimates, and derivative checks are isolated in
Appendices~\ref{app:strategy2-details} and
\ref{app:rational-monotonicity}.

\paragraph{Strategy 3: area loss.}
Normalize a unit triangle to area one, so $H$ has area six.  Each selected
boundary handoff forces a square amount of a vertex triangle outside the
local hexagonal wedge.  Two ascents, or one ascent together with a
$\Tlike$ row, force total loss greater than one; after adding the center
triangle, the available inside area is strictly less than six.

\paragraph{Strategy 4: a direct nine-point obstruction.}
In the zero-gap, all-$\Vdzero$, exact-one state, the five nonsupercritical
handoffs form one strict descent chain.  It produces common lower demands
that force six radial points and three asymmetric points into $U_C$.
Their convex hull cannot fit in an open unit equilateral triangle: an exact
caliper argument gives enclosing side at least one, whereas compact
containment in $U_C$ would give side strictly below one.

The proof uses no global noncoverage claim for $S$, no interval subdivision,
and no branch-and-bound.  The last mixed-overlap step is an exact
computer-verified Bernstein certificate; its precise mathematical input,
output, rational reduction, and digest are recorded in
Appendix~\ref{app:exact-mixed-overlap}.
