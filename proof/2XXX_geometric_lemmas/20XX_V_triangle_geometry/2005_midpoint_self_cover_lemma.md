# Midpoint Self-Cover Lemma

Status: Proven

## Statement

For a closed unit $V_i$-triangle,

$$
M_i\in T_i
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
a_i+b_i\le1.
$$

For an open triangle when the selected row-demand points are contained with a
positive margin, or under a general-position reduction that supplies this
margin,

$$
M_i\in T_i
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
a_i+b_i<1.
$$

## Normalization

Put $V_i=0$ and use the unit directions

$$
u=\left(\frac12,\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\right),
\qquad
v=\left(\frac12,-\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\right).
$$

The prescribed boundary points and radial midpoint are

$$
A=au,
\qquad
B=bv,
\qquad
M=\frac{u+v}{2}=\left(\frac12,0\right).
$$

Assume for contradiction that

$$
a+b>1.
$$

Then

$$
K=\mathrm{conv}\left\{0,A,M,B\right\}
$$

is the quadrilateral with those four vertices in cyclic order.

## Support reduction

For three outward unit normals separated by $120$ degrees, set

$$
S(\phi)=
\sum_{j=0}^2h_K\left(n_j(\phi)\right).
$$

By the exact support criterion in
[`2004_admissible_set.md`](2004_admissible_set.md), the least side length of an
equilateral triangle of orientation $\phi$ containing $K$ is

$$
L(\phi)=\frac{2}{\sqrt3}S(\phi).
$$

On every interval where the three support vertices are fixed,

$$
S''=-S\le0.
$$

Hence an angular minimum occurs at a support breakpoint. For a convex
quadrilateral, these breakpoints are the outward normals to its four edges.
It is therefore enough to evaluate the edges

$$
0A,
\qquad
AM,
\qquad
MB,
\qquad
B0.
$$

## The edges through the origin

For the outward normal to $0A$, direct projection gives

$$
S_{0A}=
\max\left(\frac{\sqrt3}{4},\frac{\sqrt3 a}{2}\right)
+\frac{\sqrt3 b}{2}.
$$

If $a\ge1/2$, then

$$
S_{0A}=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}(a+b)>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

If $a<1/2$, then $b>1-a>1/2$, and

$$
S_{0A}=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}(1+2b)>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

The edge $B0$ is symmetric, so

$$
S_{B0}>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

## The edge $AM$

Set

$$
D_a=\sqrt{4a^2-2a+1}.
$$

Projection onto the three normals generated by the outward normal to $AM$
gives

$$
S_{AM}=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\sqrt3(a+b)}{2D_a},&0\le a\le\dfrac12,\\
\dfrac{\sqrt3(2a^2+b)}{2D_a},&\dfrac12<a\le1.
\end{cases}
$$

If $a\le1/2$, then $D_a\le1$, so

$$
S_{AM}\ge\frac{\sqrt3(a+b)}2>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

If $a>1/2$, then $b>1-a$. Also

$$
\left(2a^2-a+1\right)^2-D_a^2
=a^2(2a-1)^2\ge0.
$$

Therefore

$$
2a^2+b>2a^2-a+1\ge D_a,
$$

and again

$$
S_{AM}>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

The edge $MB$ is symmetric and satisfies the same strict bound.

## Conclusion

Every possible angular minimum has

$$
S(\phi)>\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$

Thus every enclosing equilateral triangle has side length

$$
L(\phi)=\frac{2}{\sqrt3}S(\phi)>1,
$$

contrary to the existence of a closed unit triangle containing
$0,A,M,B$. Hence

$$
M_i\in T_i\implies a_i+b_i\le1.
$$

If the selected demand points are contained with positive margin and equality
$a_i+b_i=1$ held, both edge demands could be increased slightly while keeping
the four points inside the triangle. The closed result would then be violated.
This proves the stated strict corollary. The closed theorem itself does not
silently turn arbitrary supremal reach coordinates into strict inequalities.
